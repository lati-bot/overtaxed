## How Texas Property Tax Protests Work

### The Three-Phase Process

**Phase 1: File Your Protest**
- Deadline: May 15 or 30 days after notice mailed, whichever is later
- File Form 50-132 (Notice of Protest) or use county's online portal
- Check BOTH boxes: "Value is over market value" AND "Value is unequal compared with other properties"
- Submit your opinion of value (the number you believe is correct)

**Phase 2: Informal Hearing/Settlement**
- After filing, most counties offer an informal review with an appraiser
- Some counties (Harris) use automated settlement systems (iSettle)
- This is where ~60-70% of protests are resolved
- The appraiser reviews your evidence and may offer a settlement
- If you accept, the protest ends at the new agreed value
- **Critical**: Evidence submitted BEFORE the informal hearing is key

**Phase 3: Formal ARB Hearing**
- If informal doesn't resolve, you go before a 3-member Appraisal Review Board panel
- Hearings typically last 15-20 minutes (some counties limit to 15 min)
- You present evidence first, then the CAD appraiser responds
- Bring 4-5 copies of all evidence (one per ARB member + appraiser + yourself)
- The ARB issues a binding decision for that tax year
- Can be attended in person, by phone, or video conference

### Key Legal Protections
- **Your value CANNOT be raised** as a result of protesting (Texas Tax Code)
- **Burden of proof is on the CAD** if you submit an independent appraisal before the hearing
- You can protest every year, regardless of whether value went up
- Filing a protest costs nothing â€” it's a constitutional right

## The Two Legal Grounds That Win

### Ground 1: Market Value (Â§41.43)
**What it is**: Your property's appraised value exceeds what it would sell for on the open market as of January 1.

**Evidence needed**:
- 3-5 recent arms-length sales of comparable properties

- Properties within 0.5-1 mile radius, same neighborhood/school district preferred
- Similar in: square footage (Â±20%), year built (Â±10 years), beds/baths, lot size, quality/condition

### Ground 2: Uniform & Equal / Unequal Appraisal (Â§42.26)
**What it is**: Your property is appraised higher per square foot than comparable properties currently on the tax roll â€” even if the market value might be accurate.

**This is the MORE POWERFUL argument for most Texas residential protests because**:
- Uses publicly available CAD data (appraised values are public, unlike sales)
- Doesn't require access to MLS/sales data
- The equity argument can reduce value even when market values are rising
- Texas Tax Code Â§42.26 specifically provides this remedy

**Evidence needed**:
- 5-10 comparable properties from the same tax roll
- Must be "appropriately adjusted" for differences
- Calculate the MEDIAN adjusted appraised value of the comps
- If your appraised value exceeds this median, you're entitled to relief
- The 10% threshold: If your appraisal ratio exceeds the median by at least 10%, relief is mandatory

**Format â€” The Comp Equity Grid**:
This is the standard format that CADs, ARBs, and professional firms all use:

```
SUBJECT PROPERTY: [Address] | Account: [#] | Appraised Value: $XXX,XXX | Sqft: X,XXX | $/sqft: $XXX

```

**Key adjustment factors**:
- Size ($/sqft Ã— difference in sqft)
- Age (newer = higher, older = lower)
- Quality/grade (from CAD records)
- Condition (from CAD records)
- Pool, garage, extra features
- Lot size (if significantly different)

### Dual Argument Strategy (Best Practice)
**Always file on BOTH grounds**. Texas Tax Code requires the value to be set at the LOWEST value indicated by any ground. Professional firms always present both arguments. Your package should support both.

## Evidence Format Best Practices

### What ARBs Want to See

Based on research across all 10 counties and professional firm standards:

1. **One-Page Summary / Cover Sheet**
   - Property address and account number
   - Current appraised value
   - Your opinion of value (the number you want)
   - Brief statement of grounds (market value + unequal appraisal)
   - List of attached evidence

2. **Equity Comp Grid** (1-2 pages)
   - 5-10 comparable properties from the CAD database
   - All adjustments shown with math
   - Median calculation clearly shown
   - Subject property highlighted or in separate column

3. **Sales Comp Analysis** (1-2 pages)
   - 3-5 recent sales with adjusted values
   - Map showing locations relative to subject
   - Source of sales data cited

4. **Property Condition Evidence** (if applicable)
   - Timestamped photos
   - Contractor repair estimates on letterhead
   - Description of deferred maintenance

5. **Property Record Corrections** (if applicable)
   - Printout from CAD showing errors
   - Correct information with supporting docs
   - Highlight specific errors (sqft, bed/bath, etc.)

### Format Requirements
- **PDF is universal** â€” all county portals accept PDF uploads
- Most portals also accept JPG/PNG for photos
- Bring **4-5 printed copies** to formal ARB hearings
- Keep total package concise: **8-12 pages max** for residential
- Number all pages for reference during hearing
- Use tables and charts â€” ARB members see dozens of cases per day

### Common Mistakes That Get Protests Denied
1. **No evidence at all** â€” just saying "it's too high" without proof
2. **Emotional arguments** â€” "I can't afford this" or "taxes are unfair" (ARB only sets value, not rates)
3. **Using Zillow estimates** as evidence (not accepted as reliable)
4. **Wrong comps** â€” using properties too dissimilar (different neighborhood, size, age)
5. **No adjustments** â€” presenting raw comps without adjusting for differences
6. **Missing deadline** â€” after May 15, you're locked out for the year
7. **Not checking both protest boxes** â€” filing only on market value misses the equity argument
8. **Discussing tax rates or tax amounts** â€” ARB only controls value, not rates

### Is $/sqft Enough?
**No, but it's the foundation.** The raw $/sqft comparison gets you 70% of the way there. But to survive scrutiny at an ARB hearing (and to match what professional firms submit), you need adjustments for:
- Size differences (larger homes generally have lower $/sqft)
- Age differences
- Quality/condition differences
- Feature differences (pool, garage, etc.)

Professional firms make these adjustments. Your $49 package should too.

### Do You Need Photos?
**Not in the evidence package â€” but guide the homeowner to take them.** Photos of property condition issues (deferred maintenance, damage, outdated features) are submitted BY THE HOMEOWNER as supplementary evidence. Your package should:
1. Audit the CAD's condition rating vs. likely reality
2. Provide a "Photo Guide" telling homeowner exactly what to photograph
3. Explain how to get repair estimates
4. Show where/how to upload photos to their county's portal

### Do You Need Sales Data?
**Ideally yes, but equity comps alone can win.** Due to Texas's non-disclosure law, getting actual sale prices is harder than in other states. Options:
1. CAD's own sales data (available in some evidence packets)
2. Partnership with a licensed real estate agent
3. Listing price data from public sources (weaker but usable)
4. The homeowner's own purchase price (if recent)

**Recommendation**: Build the package primarily around equity comps (publicly available CAD data), but include sales analysis where data is available. The equity argument is actually STRONGER in Texas because it sidesteps the non-disclosure issue entirely.
