Travis County Appraisal District (TCAD) released preliminary 2026 property values in late March, and the numbers tell a story of continued growth across Austin — but not evenly distributed.

**The headline:** Most Austin homeowners saw their appraised value increase by 8-12% from 2025 levels, adding an average of **$20,000-40,000** to their assessed value. For a typical home, that translates to roughly **$400-800 more per year** in property taxes.

But some areas got hit much harder than others. Here's what we found by analyzing the data across Austin's major zip codes.

## Why These Increases Happened

Several factors drove the 2026 assessment increases:

**1. Sales Data Lag**  
TCAD uses sales from 2024-2025 to set 2026 values. Austin's market stayed strong through 2025, with continued demand and limited inventory pushing prices up.

**2. Construction Costs**  
Building materials and labor costs remained elevated, affecting replacement cost calculations for existing homes.

**3. Infrastructure Investment**  
New transit projects (particularly around downtown and east Austin) influenced projected property values.

**4. Comparative Market Analysis**  
Areas that lagged in previous years (like some east Austin neighborhoods) saw "catch-up" adjustments.

## What This Means for Your Property Taxes

Your actual tax bill depends on more than just your appraised value. Here's the math:

**Property Tax = Assessed Value × Tax Rate × (1 - Exemptions)**

For 2026, the average combined tax rate across Austin-area taxing jurisdictions is approximately **2.0%** (this varies by location — some areas are 1.8%, others reach 2.3%).

So if your home's value increased by $30,000:
- **Tax increase**: $30,000 × 0.020 = **$600/year**
- **Monthly impact**: **$50 more per month**

Don't forget your **homestead exemption** can reduce this. If you haven't filed for homestead, you're leaving money on the table.

## Your Options: What You Can Do Right Now

### Option 1: Accept the Increase

If your home's new value seems reasonable compared to recent sales in your neighborhood, you might choose to accept it. Check what similar homes sold for in 2024-2025 on Zillow or HAR.com.

### Option 2: File a Protest

If your property seems over-valued compared to similar homes, you can protest. **The deadline is May 15, 2026.**

Here's when protesting makes sense:
- Your value increased more than 12% without major improvements
- Similar homes in your neighborhood have lower appraised values
- Recent sales in your area suggest a lower market value
- Your home has condition issues not reflected in the assessment

## How to Check Your Neighbors

Before deciding whether to protest, see how your property compares:

1. **Go to TCAD.org** and search your address
2. **Note your 2026 appraised value**  
3. **Search 3-5 similar homes** in your immediate area (same age, size, condition)
4. **Compare the $/square foot** values

If your property is appraised significantly higher per square foot than similar homes, you have grounds for a protest.

## TCAD Protest Process: What to Expect

Travis County has a two-step process:

**Step 1: Informal Hearing**
- File online at traviscad.org by May 15
- Upload your evidence (comparable sales, photos, etc.)
- TCAD reviews and may adjust your value
- This resolves about 60% of cases

**Step 2: Formal ARB Hearing** (if needed)
- If informal doesn't work, your case goes to the Appraisal Review Board
- You present your case to a 3-person panel
- Hearings typically happen June-August
- Bring printed evidence and be prepared to speak for 3-5 minutes
